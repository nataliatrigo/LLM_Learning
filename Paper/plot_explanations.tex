\section{Model Objects Used in the Plots}

The state in the simulation is the user's history with Seller A,
\[
    (S,F),
\]
where $S$ is the number of successes previously observed from Seller A and $F$
is the number of failures. Because the user does not observe Seller A's hidden
product choice, the user updates a misspecified single-product belief,
\[
    \theta \mid S,F \sim \operatorname{Beta}(1+S,1+F).
\]
The probability that the Thompson-sampling user chooses Seller A is
\[
    \rho(S,F)
    =
    \Pr(\tilde{\theta} \ge p_0 \mid S,F),
    \qquad
    \tilde{\theta} \sim \operatorname{Beta}(1+S,1+F),
\]
where $p_0$ is the known success probability of Seller B.

For product $x \in \{1,2\}$, define Seller A's product-specific action value as
\[
    Q_x(S,F)
    =
    R-c_x
    +
    \gamma \left[
        p_x V(S+1,F)
        +
        (1-p_x)V(S,F+1)
    \right].
\]
The optimal product at state $(S,F)$ is the product with the larger $Q$ value.
Equivalently, product 2 is optimal when
\[
    Q_2(S,F) - Q_1(S,F) \ge 0.
\]
Since
\[
    Q_2(S,F)-Q_1(S,F)
    =
    -(c_2-c_1)
    +
    \gamma(p_2-p_1)
    \left[V(S+1,F)-V(S,F+1)\right],
\]
product 2 is optimal exactly when
\[
    D(S,F)
    \equiv
    V(S+1,F)-V(S,F+1)
    \ge
    \frac{c_2-c_1}{\gamma(p_2-p_1)}.
\]
The value-difference plots visualize this threshold condition directly.

\section{Best Response as p0 Varies}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{best_response_by_p0.png}
    \caption{Summary of Seller A's best response as Seller B's known success probability $p_0$ varies.}
    \label{fig:best-response-by-p0}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:best-response-by-p0} summarizes how Seller A's incentives change
with $p_0$.

\begin{itemize}
    \item The top-left panel plots $Q_2(0,0)-Q_1(0,0)$ at the initial state.
    Positive values mean product 2 is optimal at the prior.
    \item The top-right panel compares initial demand for A with simulated market
    share. Initial demand is high when $p_0$ is low and falls as Seller B becomes
    stronger.
    \item The bottom-left panel compares product 2 use across solved policy
    states and simulated histories.
    \item The bottom-right panel shows that Seller A's average profit falls as
    $p_0$ increases, because A is chosen less often and must sometimes pay for
    the more expensive product to protect reputation.
\end{itemize}

The key pattern is hump-shaped: product 2 is most attractive when Seller A is
neither safely ahead nor hopelessly behind. If $p_0$ is very low, Seller B is
weak, so Seller A can often save cost with product 1. If $p_0$ is very high,
the chance of future demand from A is too low to justify much investment. For
intermediate $p_0$, a success can meaningfully improve future demand, so product
2 is more valuable.

\section{Policy Heatmaps in Count Space}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{best_response_policy_heatmaps.png}
    \caption{Optimal product choice over the raw count state space $(S,F)$.}
    \label{fig:count-policy}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:count-policy} plots the optimal product for Seller A over the
state space $(S,F)$.

\begin{itemize}
    \item The horizontal axis is the number of observed successes $S$.
    \item The vertical axis is the number of observed failures $F$.
    \item Yellow means product 1, the cheap product.
    \item Green means product 2, the high-quality product.
    \item The black dot is the initial state $(0,0)$.
    \item The gray region is infeasible under the truncation $S+F \le N$.
\end{itemize}

The green regions are states where a success is sufficiently valuable for future
demand. These regions are not simply ``bad reputation'' or ``good reputation''
states. Instead, they tend to occur near reputation margins where the next
outcome can move the user's belief enough to change future choice probabilities.

\section{Policy Over Posterior State Space}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{best_response_policy_posterior_state_space.png}
    \caption{Optimal product choice over posterior mean and posterior uncertainty.}
    \label{fig:posterior-policy}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:posterior-policy} re-expresses the same policy in posterior
belief coordinates. Each point is still a count state $(S,F)$, but it is plotted
using
\[
    m(S,F) = \mathbb{E}[\theta \mid S,F]
    =
    \frac{1+S}{2+S+F}
\]
on the horizontal axis and the posterior standard deviation on the vertical
axis.

The dashed vertical line is $p_0$. It is the known success probability of Seller
B and therefore the Thompson-sampling comparison threshold. The black dot is the
initial prior $\operatorname{Beta}(1,1)$, which has posterior mean $0.5$ and
standard deviation $\sqrt{1/12}$.

The fan shape is mechanical. As $S+F$ grows, the posterior becomes more precise,
so points move downward. At the same time, different success rates generate
different posterior means, spreading points horizontally. The upper part of the
fan corresponds to low-count, high-uncertainty states; the lower part corresponds
to high-count, precise states.

\section{Value-Difference Diagnostic}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{best_response_value_difference_posterior_state_space_interior.png}
    \caption{Interior diagnostic for the value-difference threshold $D(S,F)$.}
    \label{fig:value-difference}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:value-difference} plots
\[
    D(S,F)
    -
    \frac{c_2-c_1}{\gamma(p_2-p_1)}.
\]
Positive values mean product 2 is optimal, while negative values mean product 1
is optimal. This plot is useful because it shows the smooth object behind the
policy rule. The policy plot discretizes states into yellow and green, while
the value-difference plot shows whether those regions come from a coherent
threshold or from numerical noise.

The displayed version uses the interior restriction $S+F \le 90$, while the
baseline value iteration solves the grid up to $N=100$. This removes the top
boundary band and helps check whether the visible policy pattern is driven by
the artificial truncation.

\section{One-Step Lookahead Heuristic}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{one_step_heuristic_comparison_by_p0.png}
    \caption{Comparison between the full dynamic-programming policy and the
    one-step lookahead heuristic.}
    \label{fig:one-step-comparison}
\end{figure}

The one-step lookahead heuristic avoids solving the full dynamic program.
Instead, it approximates the value of an additional success by the immediate
change in the user's future demand probability:
\[
    \rho(S+1,F)-\rho(S,F+1).
\]
The heuristic uses product 2 when
\[
    c_2-c_1
    \le
    \gamma(p_2-p_1)\kappa
    \left[
        \rho(S+1,F)-\rho(S,F+1)
    \right],
\]
where $\kappa$ converts one unit of extra demand into dollars. In the default
simulation, $\kappa=(R-c_1)/(1-\gamma)$, the discounted value of a stream of
cheap-product margins. This can be changed from the command line with
\texttt{--lookahead-demand-value}.

Figure~\ref{fig:one-step-comparison} compares this heuristic with the solved
best response. The agreement plot shows how often the heuristic picks the same
product as the dynamic program. The product-2-use plot compares how much
reputation investment each rule recommends after weighting states by demand.
The disagreement plot separates two types of mistakes: states where the
heuristic uses product 2 but the DP does not, and states where the DP uses
product 2 but the heuristic does not.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{one_step_heuristic_agreement_heatmaps_interior.png}
    \caption{Interior state-space comparison between the one-step heuristic and
    the full dynamic-programming policy.}
    \label{fig:one-step-agreement}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:one-step-agreement} shows where the heuristic matches or misses
the DP policy. Yellow and green are agreement regions. Red indicates states
where the heuristic recommends product 2 but the DP recommends product 1. Blue
indicates states where the DP recommends product 2 but the heuristic recommends
product 1.

The current default heuristic captures much of the policy geometry, but it is
more aggressive than the dynamic program when $p_0$ is very high. This makes
sense: the heuristic only sees the immediate demand jump from one success, while
the full DP also accounts for how hard it is to maintain reputation against a
strong outside option.

\section{Truncation Robustness}

The infinite state space is approximated by solving states with $S+F \le N$.
The baseline plots use $N=100$. To check whether the boundary condition is
driving the policy, the code re-solves the model at $N=150$ and $N=200$ and
compares policy choices on the common state space.

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Policy disagreement when comparing the baseline $N=100$ solution to larger truncations.}
\label{tab:robustness}
\begin{tabular}{llrr}
\toprule
Comparison $N$ & States compared & Max disagreement & Mean disagreement \\
\midrule
150 & All common states, $S+F \le 100$ & 1.0095\% & 0.3163\% \\
150 & Safe interior, $S+F \le 90$ & 0.1911\% & 0.0576\% \\
200 & All common states, $S+F \le 100$ & 1.0095\% & 0.3163\% \\
200 & Safe interior, $S+F \le 90$ & 0.1911\% & 0.0576\% \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The main conclusion is that the policy is very stable away from the truncation
edge. Disagreements are concentrated near the artificial boundary $S+F=N$.
The high-uncertainty points in the posterior-state plots are not boundary
states: high posterior uncertainty comes from low $S+F$, whereas the truncation
boundary is at high $S+F$.

\section{Simulation Path Plot}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{simulation_paths_by_p0.png}
    \caption{Simulated demand and product 2 usage paths for selected values of $p_0$.}
    \label{fig:simulation-paths}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:simulation-paths} shows simulated histories under the solved
best-response policy. The top panel tracks Seller A's market share over time.
The bottom panel tracks the probability that Seller A uses product 2 conditional
on being chosen.

As $p_0$ increases, Seller A is chosen less often. Conditional on being chosen,
product 2 use tends to rise over the intermediate range because reputation
investment becomes more important. At very high $p_0$, demand is so low that
investment is often not worthwhile.
